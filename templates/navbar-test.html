Our Findings

1. Stacked Line Chart

The International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN) collects valuable research from around the world to provide and continually improve information on endangered species. Each category on The IUCN Red List notes the reasons for the change recorded, which allows us to quickly identify species that are genuinely improving or deteriorating in status. Plant species are moving towards increased extinction risk most rapidly, while vertebrates are the second most threatened group. We see from the graph that the least affected group is of fungi and protists. This may be because fewer studies on those species are performed in general, as there is no data available for these species in the records until 2002.

2. Heatmap

Direct threats to species are the proximate human activities and processes that have impacted, are impacting, or may impact the status of the taxon being assessed (e.g., unsustainable fishing or logging). Threats can be past (historical, unlikely to return; or historical, likely to return), ongoing, and/or likely to occur in the future. The present heatmap show the regions with most number of threatened species. Ecuador is a hotspot for species endangerment, with by far the highest number of threatened species due to its large number of endangered plants. Following closely in rank are India, Madagascar and Tanzania.

3. Scatter Plot

Reserve networks figures prominently claim conservation strategies that aim to reduce extinction rates. We test this claim by plotting the protected areas and deforestation against the number of species at risk in 208 countries. We compared numbers of threatened mammals, birds, higher plants and fungi species at risk included in existing countries to with the percentage of protected reserves and the amount of annual deforestation within the country. We indeed saw that some of the countries with more threatened species, like New Caledonia and Slovenia, did have a significant amount of conserved area and lower amount of deforestation. However some countries like US and Australia, which have high numbers of threatened species, have a lesser protected area. Overall, the correlation between conservation efforts and endangerment levels is unclear, likely due to the negative-feedback-loop nature of conservation versus endangerment.
